Why compare countries with similar geography?

Much of APAC shares a tropical rainforest climate and broadly similar sea–land configurations (peninsulas, archipelagos, long coastlines). By holding these geographic factors roughly constant, we can ask a cleaner question: How do rising land–ocean temperatures translate into climate hazards (heat, intense rainfall, coastal flooding), and how does each country’s development and capacity turn hazards into disasters—or prevent them?

APAC Risk & Vulnerability

Among geographically similar neighbors, Malaysia sits at the lower end of the composite risk spectrum. This contrast is instructive: with climate and location broadly comparable across the Maritime Continent. The differences we see on the map are largely about Vulnerability—infrastructure, governance, early warning systems, and social protection. The global signal is clear: land warms faster than ocean, and recent decades sit well above the 20th-century baseline. In APAC, this warming baseline is further modulated by Pacific variability . Even when today’s risk index looks moderate, a warmer baseline can intensify impacts when events occur.

Zoom on Malaysia: similar climate, lower risk

In a neighborhood sharing tropical monsoons and strong ocean influence, Malaysia’s comparatively lower risk index suggests that vulnerability management has made a difference. The strategic implication under global warming is twofold: (1) keep vulnerability from drifting upward as hazards intensify (, and (2) monitor how the land–ocean warming gap and Pacific swings shape compound events (heat + heavy rain, storm surge + sea-level rise). Many APAC countries can similarly maintain or reduce vulnerability with steady adaptation—even as the physical climate shifts.

Key takeaways

  • Control for geography: With similar climate and coastal settings, vulnerability explains most cross-country risk gaps.
  • Warming baseline matters: Land warms faster than ocean; Pacific variability modulates APAC hazards.
  • Malaysia’s lesson: Lower risk amid similar geography shows adaptation works; the task is to keep capacity ahead of shifting hazards.
